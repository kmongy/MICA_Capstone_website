A Place to Call Home

An analysis of Immigrant and Refugee Oral Histories

This is May.

May is a first generation immigrant.

"I was born in Laos in 1962. I have 9 siblings. I'm the fifth oldest child in my family. My two older brothers passed away when they were only four and six years old.

Before my father passed, we lived a pretty comfortable life. He was what was considered a personal bodyguard for General Vang Pao. I remember having couches and sleeping on mattresses. I don't think many other people had things like this. We didn't have to really do anything.

But my father passed when I was 8 years old. Life changed immediatlely after that. All the people that helped us before had left. We were left to do things by ourselves. We didn't even know how to plant or farm well."

Immigrants and refugees in the United States have always had stories to tell. Their stories have shaped and continue shaping history.

We often only hear about immigrant communities:

What if we could hear from immigrant and refugee communities instead?

Remembering and retelling oral histories are essential for immigrant and refugee communities. It's how traditions and culture are passed down to each generation.

"Outside of school, sometimes you still have to help your teacher with farming. We had a deep respect for our teachers and so when they asked for help, you'd volunteer to plant the rice or carry vegetables. At home, we'd help my mother with farming too.

We raised some chickens and pigs. I even had a pet pig. I didn't want to kill it to eat it. I had to watch my younger siblings too. We never stayed home to play or anything. There was a lot to do. Life was pretty difficult. We learned quickly that if your parents didn't have status or have a good job, that you'd be left to do everything by yourself."

Each story reveals something about the journey that include struggles and triumphs. Along with May's story, 20 other oral histories were analyzed for this project. Sentiment

Like any story, oral histories have positive and negative elements that comprise the whole.


The words used to describe their experiences were passed through a set of sentiment lexicons. The AFINN lexicon assigns a word a rating between -5 and 5, indicating the intensity of a negative or positive sentiment.

Some oral histories were a little shorter than others, but the words used to describe their experiences offer insight into their world.

When accounting for the stories as a whole, each person was given an average sentiment score.

**ADD average sentiment scores **

All together, the average sentiment for all the stories combined is -0.035; a slightly negative sentiment, but essentially neutral overall.

"I remember walking to school with a little can of charcoal to warm our hands during the cold season. We didn't have lunch at school. You'd have to walk all the way back home or if you had the means to pack lunch, you'd do that. When you got to school, you'd leave that can next to you.

There were these rules in place where your nails couldn't be too long. If it was, your hands would be hit. For all the school subjects, we had to memorize or write it down in our notebooks. We didn't have books to bring home. If the teacher asked, we had to be ready to respond with what we memorized. That's how we got credit. We memorized so much that there are some things I still remember to this day."
