Memories We Carry

Remembering Immigrant, Refugee, and Migrant Oral Histories


Remembering Stories

Remembering and retelling oral histories are part of the human experience. It's how traditions and culture are passed from generation to generation. Retelling stories reminds the community of where one comes from and where one might go, holding together both the past and the future.


This is especially true for for immigrants, refugees, asylum seekers, and migrants. My parents were refugees, and would chuckle through their stories when they tell me about their childhood adventures, or cry through the sharing of difficult experiences. By retelling their stories, they'd also insert a lesson or two about the difficulties and triumphs of life. In each of their retellings, I learned more about them and more about who I am because of them.


As they remember and describe their experiences to me, I try to hold on to every word. Remembering and retelling stories—histories—is part and parcel of the immigrant life.


I invite you to remember.

This is May. She was a refugee from Thailand.

Read along as she retells her story.

"I was born in Laos in 1962. I have 11 siblings. I'm the fifth oldest child in my family. My two older brothers passed away when they were only four and six years old. I also had two sisters after me that passed away as infants.

Before my father passed, we lived a pretty comfortable life. He was what was considered a personal bodyguard for General Vang Pao. I remember having couches and sleeping on mattresses. I don't think many other people had things like this. We didn't have to really do anything.

But my father passed when I was 8 years old. Life changed immediately after that. All the people that helped us before had left. We were left to do things by ourselves. We didn't even know how to plant or farm well because my mother was married so young. My father was a teacher before he became a soldier. He was well respected by our community. A lot of people helped our family because of who he was."

"I remember walking to school with a little can of charcoal to warm our hands during the cold season. We didn't have lunch at school. You'd have to walk all the way back home or if you had the means to pack lunch, you'd do that. When you got to school, you'd leave that can next to you.

There were these rules in place where your nails couldn't be too long. If it was, your hands would be hit. For all the school subjects, we had to memorize or write it down in our notebooks. We didn't have books to bring home. If the teacher asked, we had to be ready to respond with what we memorized. That's how we got credit. We memorized so much that there are some things I still remember to this day."

Remembering Feelings

Positive and negative feelings are captured in the retelling of oral histories.


To get a sense of the sentiment, all the oral histories were passed through a set of sentiment lexicons. The lexicons are based on English words created through various means of crowdsourcing and based on what are called 'unigrams'—a single word.


The oral histories were passed through a lexicon where each word is given a rating between -5 and 5 indicating the intensity of a negative or positive sentiment.

Remembering Connections

The words used to describe one's experience are like a constellation, pointing to a north star revealing centers of thought.


Some word connections are off on their own, while other words have many interconnections creating little worlds that require investigation.


Though helpful to gauge general sentiment, the lexicons above revealed emotions and feelings based on single words out of context. To get a better grasp of the story, you have to notice the relationships between the words.

By highlighting word pairs, the context starts become a lot more clear.

Some words have many connections while others are unique to a person or a situation.

"The United States did send an aircraft to transport our people. The first aircrafts were only for the generals and leaders that had direct contact with the CIA. When those aircrafts landed, we all tried to get on board, even though we knew it couldn't carry all of us. If you had a father, it was easier for you and your family to get on." "When all the Hmong leaders were airlifted off, we never saw another plane come back to get the rest of us. This was when we knew we had to find our own way out to Thailand from where we were in LongCheng. LongCheng was a village that became an army base. We were scared that the communists would come here first to kill us. That's why we all wanted to leave so badly to Thailand."

Remembering History

The whole story is meant to be remembered and passed on. It's helpful to have some kind of device to assist with remembering those stories.


The Hmong story cloth is one way to intentionally remember history.

Created in refugee camps, the story cloths were originally created to sell. It became much more years after the Vietnam war.

The story cloth was one way the Hmong people chose to remember their experiences during and after the Vietnam War.

Other groups wrote down their stories or, as we've seen, passed their stories on through the telling of oral histories.

"LongCheng became a ghost town pretty quick. It was so quiet. All those who didn't get on the plane quickly left to other villages, away from LongCheng. All those that stayed were those who were orphaned or were without a father to lead. It was such a hard and sad time for us. Everyone was scared. Especially when it came to night time. Everything was left behind. All the animals, like the chickens and the pigs were just left wandering. What you wanted the most was to leave this place.

We were one of the families that were left here. My father died in 1970. It was my mother with her 7 children. My older sister was in school, so it was me and other sister, who were preteens who were helping with everything. We didn't know what to do to get out. My mother was not educated. She didn't even know the Laotian language. At that time, you were supposed to know that language. We just stayed and were scared. It wasn't until much later when my older sister came back to find us in order for us to leave."
