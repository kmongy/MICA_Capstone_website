Memories We Carry

An Analysis of Immigrant, Refugee, and Migrant Oral Histories

Remembering Stories

Remembering and retelling oral histories are part of the human experience. It's how traditions and culture are passed from generation to generation. Retelling stories reminds the community of where one comes from and where one might go, holding together both the past and the future.


This is especially true for immigrants, refugees, asylum seekers, and migrants. My parents were refugees, and would chuckle through their stories when they tell me about their childhood adventures or cry through the sharing of difficult experiences. By retelling their stories, they'd also insert a lesson or two about the difficulties and triumphs of life.


As they remember and describe their experiences to me, I try to hold on to every word. Remembering and retelling stories—histories—is part and parcel of the immigrant life.


As I was listening again to my mother retell her story, I wondered if I could gain some more insight into her life by breaking down her story into its atomic parts and then building it back up again—from single words, back to sentences, and then into the full story. I also wondered about the words that were used to describe her experiences and if it would clue me into something I may have missed before.

My mother's name is May, and she was a refugee from Laos.

In the interest of remembering, you'll have the opportunity to read a transcription of my mother's oral history.

Read along as she retells her story.

"I was born in Laos in 1962. I have 11 siblings. I'm the fifth oldest child in my family. My two older brothers passed away when they were only four and six years old. I also had two sisters after me that passed away as infants.

Before my father passed, we lived a pretty comfortable life. He was what was considered a personal bodyguard for General Vang Pao. I remember having couches and sleeping on mattresses. I don't think many other people had things like this. We didn't have to really do anything.

But my father passed when I was 8 years old. Life changed immediately after that. All the people that helped us before had left. We were left to do things by ourselves. We didn't even know how to plant or farm well because my mother was married so young. My father was a teacher before he became a soldier. He was well respected by our community. A lot of people helped our family because of who he was."

Remembering Words

Oral histories reveal struggles, triumphs, and everything in between. The words used to describe these experiences may unearth topics or themes that are important to the person telling the oral history.


Including my mother, I wanted to find out what immigrants, refugees, and migrants were remembering in their stories. I analyzed a total of 21 oral histories from different immigrant and refugee groups. All the words were then separated into single words, amounting to 77,321 words.


However, not every word needed to be analyzed. So, after accounting for common stop words like "and", "but", "the", and others, the total word count became 20,532 words.

"I remember walking to school with a little can of charcoal to warm our hands during the cold season. We didn't have lunch at school. You'd have to walk all the way back home or if you had the means to pack lunch, you'd do that. When you got to school, you'd leave that can next to you.

There were these rules in place where your nails couldn't be too long. If it was, your hands would be hit. For all the school subjects, we had to memorize or write it down in our notebooks. We didn't have books to bring home. If the teacher asked, we had to be ready to respond with what we memorized. That's how we got credit. We memorized so much that there are some things I still remember to this day."

Remembering Feelings

Positive and negative feelings are captured in the retelling of oral histories.


To get a sense of these feelings, the oral histories were passed through a set of sentiment lexicons. The lexicons are based on English words created through various means of crowdsourcing and based on what are called 'unigrams'—a single word.


For this section, the oral histories were passed through a lexicon where each word is given a rating between -5 and 5 indicating the intensity of a negative or positive sentiment. For example, the word "danger" is given an a score of -2, and the word "opportunity" is given a score of 3.


Words from the oral histories have to match the words within the lexicon in order to be given an intensity score. I compared May's story to three others to see if there were any similarities. Notice that the chart below does not reach either extreme and goes from -4 to 4.



The Intensity and Frequency of Words

Positive and negative word sentiment values within each person's oral history.

The amount of words above and below the middle line are pretty even across everyone's story. The intensity levels of the words distributed across the oral histories in this example reveal a relatively neutral tone. I was expecting the words to display more negative sentiment, but was proven wrong about that hunch.


What I also didn't anticipate was how many times words were used. A good chunk of the oral histories, as matched with the lexicon, were used only once. This is revealed by how many light green dots there are on the charts.

Remembering Emotions

Matched with a different lexicon, words from the oral histories were categorized into positive and negative emotions with sub-categories.


Included in these sub-categories are the emotions: anger, fear, anticipation, trust, surprise, sadness, joy, and disgust.


I didn't realize at first, but words could be included in multiple categories. For example, the word "mother" was categorized under the anticipation, sadness, joy, and trust categories, making clear that a word can bring about a variety of emotions.

"The United States did send an aircraft to transport our people. The first aircrafts were only for the generals and leaders that had direct contact with the CIA. When those aircrafts landed, we all tried to get on board, even though we knew it couldn't carry all of us. If you had a father, it was easier for you and your family to get on." "When all the Hmong leaders were airlifted off, we never saw another plane come back to get the rest of us. This was when we knew we had to find our own way out to Thailand from where we were in LongCheng. LongCheng was a village that became an army base. We were scared that the communists would come here first to kill us. That's why we all wanted to leave so badly to Thailand."

Remembering History

Stories are meant to be remembered and passed on. Sometimes, it's helpful to have some kind of device to assist with remembering.


The Hmong story cloth is one way to intentionally remember history.

Created in refugee camps, the Hmong story cloths were originally created to sell to foreigners. It grew to become a device to tell and retell stories after the Vietnam War.

The story cloth served as a way to document the Hmong experience.

For all the oral histories represented in this project, each person intentionally wanted to retell their story to someone else. The act of remembering is important and not something to be missed.

"LongCheng became a ghost town pretty quick. It was so quiet. All those who didn't get on the plane quickly left to other villages, away from LongCheng. All those that stayed were those who were orphaned or were without a father to lead. It was such a hard and sad time for us. Everyone was scared. Especially when it came to night time. Everything was left behind. All the animals, like the chickens and the pigs were just left wandering. What you wanted the most was to leave this place.

We were one of the families that were left here. My father died in 1970. It was my mother with her 7 children. My older sister was in school, so it was me and other sister, who were preteens who were helping with everything. We didn't know what to do to get out. My mother was not educated. She didn't even know the Laotian language. At that time, you were supposed to know that language. We just stayed and were scared. It wasn't until much later when my older sister came back to find us in order for us to leave."

Remembering

My mother's story is a large part of mine. It's also become a part of my children's story. This passing down of history between generations is how we remember where we come from and where we will go. In each retelling, we learn more about each other and more about who we are.


I asked my mother if she thought her story had a more positive or negative narrative arc. She replied that she felt her story was just her story—neither positive or negative—and that it was true. It was another lesson she imparted to me. She reminded me that her story didn't fall neatly into "positive" or "negative" categories. What was important to her, and to me, was that the story was shared and told over and over again.


Method

The data for this project was downloaded from different universities and organizations that have dedicated oral history initiatives. I selected and downloaded oral history transcripts from the Minnesota Historical Society, the Race and Oral History Project, the UCLA Library Center for Oral History Research, and the Religion and Forced Migration Initiative.


Most of the transcripts were in a PDF format and had to be manually cleaned before analysis. Two transcripts were already in a GoogleDoc format which made it much easier to clean. During the cleaning process, I removed interviewer and interviewee names, time stamps, and corrected some misspellings. I interviewed and transcribed my mother's story. This portion of the project took the longest to complete.


After completing the cleaning, I analyzed the oral histories using R and the tidytext package. To compare the stories, I used two different sentiment lexicons: The AFINN and the nrc. The AFINN lexicon is the intensity lexicon scoring words between -5 and 5. The nrc lexicon was the lexicon that categorized words into different emotions.


All but one of the charts were created in R and made interactive with the Plotly package. The interactive node chart was made with the networkD3 R package. The packed bubble charts were created with RAWGraphs and designed in Adobe Illustrator.
