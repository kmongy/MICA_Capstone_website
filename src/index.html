Memories We Carry

Remembering Immigrant, Refugee, and Migrant Oral Histories

May was a refugee from Thailand.

Read along as she retells her story from Laos and Thailand to the United States.

"I was born in Laos in 1962. I have 11 siblings. I'm the fifth oldest child in my family. My two older brothers passed away when they were only four and six years old. I also had two sisters after me that passed away as infants.

Before my father passed, we lived a pretty comfortable life. He was what was considered a personal bodyguard for General Vang Pao. I remember having couches and sleeping on mattresses. I don't think many other people had things like this. We didn't have to really do anything.

But my father passed when I was 8 years old. Life changed immediately after that. All the people that helped us before had left. We were left to do things by ourselves. We didn't even know how to plant or farm well because my mother was married so young. My father was a teacher before he became a soldier. He was well respected by our community. A lot of people helped our family because of who he was."

Remembering and retelling oral histories are essential for immigrants, refugees, asylum seekers, and migrants. It's how traditions, culture, and experience are passed down to each generation.

By retelling stories, it reminds the community of where one comes from and where one might go, holding together both the past and the future.

"Outside of school, sometimes you still have to help your teacher with farming. We had a deep respect for our teachers and so when they asked for help, you'd volunteer to plant the rice or carry vegetables. At home, we'd help my mother with farming too.

We raised some chickens and pigs. I even had a pet pig. I didn't want to kill it to eat it. I had to watch my younger siblings too. We never stayed home to play or anything. There was a lot to do. Life was pretty difficult. We learned quickly that if your parents didn't have status or have a good job, that you'd be left to do everything by yourself."

Each story reveals struggles, triumphs, and everything in between. Along with May's story, 20 other oral histories were analyzed for this project.

"I remember walking to school with a little can of charcoal to warm our hands during the cold season. We didn't have lunch at school. You'd have to walk all the way back home or if you had the means to pack lunch, you'd do that. When you got to school, you'd leave that can next to you.

There were these rules in place where your nails couldn't be too long. If it was, your hands would be hit. For all the school subjects, we had to memorize or write it down in our notebooks. We didn't have books to bring home. If the teacher asked, we had to be ready to respond with what we memorized. That's how we got credit. We memorized so much that there are some things I still remember to this day."

Sentiment

Each oral history includes both positive and negative sentiments. Both good and bad times were remembered along with descriptions of how one felt.


To get a sense of the feelings, all the oral histories were passed through a set of sentiment lexicons. The lexicons are based on English words created through various means of crowdsourcing and only based on what are called 'unigrams'â€”a single word.


The first lexicon used for the oral histories is the AFINN lexicon, where each word is given a rating between -5 and 5 indicating the intensity of a negative or positive sentiment.

Each box represents a different person's oral history.

"It was during the fourth month in 1975 when I remember Saigon fell to the communist forces. The next month, the Pathet Lao took control of new areas - areas that encroached on our homes. We were scared. General Vang Pao, a Hmong leader involved with the CIA, knew we all had to leave. If we stayed, we knew we were going to be captured and tortured in re-education camps. We knew people who were captured, tortured, and died."

Emotional Sentiment

Using a different lexicon, words from the oral histories were categorized into positive and negative emotions.


The emotions include: anger, fear, anticipation, trust, surprise, sadness, joy, and disgust.


Words in the oral history were matched and categorized into one of those emotions.

"The United States did send an aircraft to transport our people. The first aircrafts were only for the generals and leaders that had direct contact with the CIA. When those aircrafts landed, we all tried to get on board, even though we knew it couldn't carry all of us. If you had a father, it was easier for you and your family to get on.

When all the Hmong leaders were airlifted off, we never saw another plane come back to get the rest of us. This was when we knew we had to find our own way out to Thailand from where we were in LongCheng. LongCheng was a village that became an army base. We were scared that the communists would come here first to kill us. That's why we all wanted to leave so badly to Thailand."

A Constellation of Words

The different sentiment lexicons revealed different positive and negative emotions based on single words.

However, it doesn't reveal the whole story. Words have connections and meanings associated with those connections.

The words used to describe one's experience are like a constellation, pointing to a north star revealing centers of thought.

Some words are off on their own, while other words have many interconnections creating little worlds that require some investigation.

The Whole Story

You've read some of May's story. You've seen the different sentiments attached to her words. You also made some connections between many of those words.


The whole story is meant to be remembered and passed on. Sometimes though, it's helpful to have some kind of device to help with remembering.


The Hmong story cloth is one way to intentionally remember history.

"LongCheng became a ghost town pretty quick. It was so quiet. All those who didn't get on the plane quickly left to other villages, away from LongCheng. All those that stayed were those who were orphaned or were without a father to lead. It was such a hard and sad time for us. Everyone was scared. Especially when it came to night time. Everything was left behind. All the animals, like the chickens and the pigs were just left wandering. What you wanted the most was to leave this place.

We were one of the families that were left here. My father died in 1970. It was my mother with her 7 children. My older sister was in school, so it was me and other sister, who were preteens who were helping with everything. We didn't know what to do to get out. My mother was not educated. She didn't even know the Laotian language. At that time, you were supposed to know that language. We just stayed and were scared. It wasn't until much later when my older sister came back to find us in order for us to leave."
